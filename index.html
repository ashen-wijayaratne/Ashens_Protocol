Habits I Implemented to Optimise Sleep

These habits consistently improved my sleep, recovery, and next-day cognitive performance. They are informed by research, sources like Bryan Johnson's website, personal experimentation (using Garmin Sleep Tracking), and my own preferences.


0. Reframe Your Identity

"You [must become a] professional sleeper. Make sleep your #1 priority. Nothing influences your conscious and unconscious existence more. Plan your day around sleep. It’s the most important appointment in your calendar. Sleep will make you a top performer. I realize this is very counterintuitive and contrary to current culture, but culture-encourged sleep deprivation is harming you and society." - Bryan Johnson


1. Keeping a Consistent Bed Time and Wake-up Time

The single change that improved my sleep the most was keeping a consistent sleep schedule. I determined the latest practical bedtime during any given week and the earliest required wake time, then fixed both points so that my schedule consistently allowed around 7.5–8 hours of sleep.

  • Sleep time: 23:00
  • Wake up time: 07:00

Rationale: Consistency in sleep timing is just as important as total sleep duration. The body follows a circadian rhythm regulated by an internal clock in the brain. Going to bed and waking up at the same time each day helps align this rhythm, improving overall sleep quality. It effectively tells your brain, "Okay, this is exactly when I sleep and when I wake up everyday. Do all the important jobs now before I wake up". This also helps you wake up less groggy since it allows your body to schedule for the exact sleep cycles (REM, Deep, etc.) that your body needs for max recovery


2. Reducing Phone Use before Bedtime

This one is probably the most difficult to implement but it has one of the biggest impact on sleep quality. I try to avoid using my phone for at least 30 minutes before bed, and ideally an hour. This is because the blue light emitted by screens can suppress melatonin production, making it harder to fall asleep. Instead, I use this time to do a wind-down activity which will be discussed later.

In order to help implement this habit. Starting 2 hours before I go to bed. So from 21:00 to 07:00 my phone goes into relax mode. (Learn how to set your phone to this mode here) In this mode my phone automatically turns on a blue light filter and also a grayscale filter. This not only reduces my blue light input but the gray-scale makes my phone less desirable to go onto. Additionally, in focus mode you can choose which notifications you want to receive and which you want to block. It also allows you to set a certain wallpaper during those times only I use this to put a discouraging image on it to make me not use my phone at night.


3. Adjust your food Intake

  • Finish your last meal at least four hours before going to bed. I have a google calendar notification an hour before this four hour deadline that ensures I have my dinner on time. Allowing this window gives your body enough time to digest and helps your resting heart rate drop by the time you lie down. Eating too close to bedtime places extra metabolic strain on your body, triggers blood sugar fluctuations, suppresses melatonin, and interferes with the natural drop in core body temperature. All of this ultimately disrupting sleep quality.
  • Avoid stimulants in the hours leading up to sleep. Caffeine, alcohol, and similar substances should ideally be avoided at least 8–10 hours before bedtime. Caffeine in particular has a half-life of around 6 hours, meaning a coffee consumed earlier in the day can still significantly affect your system at night. More broadly, reducing or eliminating caffeine and alcohol altogether can help stabilise energy levels and improve overall sleep quality. In the evening, opt for calming alternatives such as herbal tea or other non-stimulating drinks.

4. Optimise Your Bedroom Environment

The bedroom environment should be set up to minimise sleep disruptions.

  • Keep the room cool – ideally between 16–19°C – to support the body’s natural drop in core temperature during sleep onset.
  • Ensure complete darkness using blackout curtains or an eye mask; even small amounts of light can interfere with melatonin production.
  • Reduce noise disturbances with earplugs or a white noise machine to prevent unwanted awakenings.

5. Implement a Wind-Down Routine

A consistent pre‑sleep routine helps transition the mind and body from wakefulness to rest.

  • Avoid stimulating work, problem‑solving, or high‑focus activities in the hour before bed.
  • Spend the final 30–60 minutes on low‑stimulation activities such as reading or light stretching.
  • The goal is to lower resting heart rate (RHR) before sleep – a key predictor of overall sleep quality.

6. Track your Sleep and Adjust

Objective data provides a more reliable basis for refining sleep habits than subjective feelings alone.

  • Monitor sleep duration and consistency to ensure adequate, regular rest.
  • Track trends in resting heart rate (RHR) and HRV as indicators of recovery and sleep quality.
  • Adjust habits based on measurable results using wearables or, as a less accurate alternative, phone‑based apps that monitor bedroom disturbances.

Relax Mode on Your Phone

iPhone (iOS)

  • Go to Settings → Focus → Sleep (or create a custom Focus)
  • Schedule it from 21:00 → 07:00
  • Enable Sleep Mode / Do Not Disturb
  • Enable Reduce White Point / Night Shift to reduce blue light
  • Optionally, set a calming wallpaper or grayscale filter while active

Android

  • Go to Settings → Digital Wellbeing & Parental Controls → Bedtime Mode
  • Set schedule: 21:00 → 07:00
  • Enable Grayscale / Blue Light Filter
  • Enable Do Not Disturb or custom Focus rules
  • Optionally, change wallpaper or use “Wind Down” features to discourage phone use

During this time, your phone will automatically reduce blue light, make the display grayscale, limit notifications, and allow you to have a focus-oriented wallpaper to avoid distractions.
